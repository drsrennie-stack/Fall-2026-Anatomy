Section 2

Course description and outcomes.

BIO 004 studies the structural organization of the human body from cell to organism. You will work in three modes: microscopic study of prepared slides, gross (macroscopic) anatomy, and examination of prosected human material.

This is pure anatomy. The focus is structure, what it is, where it is, what it looks like, how it relates to what is around it. Clinical context is threaded in so the anatomy connects to real practice.

By the end, you should be able to:

  1. Identify and distinguish tissues using currently recognized criteria.
  2. Describe the microscopic and gross anatomy of the body's major systems.
  3. Identify, in the lab, the major microscopic and gross structures of those systems.

Advisory: BIO 016 and BIO 016L are recommended but not required.

Section 3

How this course works.

Each week you'll have the full concept lecture online and a guided worksheet. Class time is where you work through it together and make it stick.

What a class day looks like

  • Most days, guided lecture. We work through the high-yield concepts, the ones that matter most, not every single detail, and you fill in your worksheet as we go. A few quick check-in questions run during the lecture to keep everyone on track, studying the night before means higher scores on them. The complete detail lives in the online lecture and your worksheet.
  • Some days, active learning. Instead of lecture, we run activities and games that make the anatomy stick.
  • A few days, TBL. Several times across the term you'll do a Team-Based Learning session covering that module's material (what that is, below).

And lab runs most class days. The online lecture and worksheet are your prep, look them over the night before and class makes far more sense.

What's a TBL?

Team-Based Learning. You take a short readiness quiz on your own first, then take the same quiz again with your team and talk through the answers. It covers that module's material and counts toward your grade, it rewards preparing and learning from your teammates. You keep the same team all term.

The one habit that makes it work: look over the lecture and worksheet before class. Come in prepared and the check questions, activities, TBLs, and lab all click.

Team-Based Learning, 30%

  • 9 TBLs across the term, done with your assigned team on TBL days.
  • iRAT (individual) then tRAT (same questions, with your team). Your individual prep drives the score.
  • Application and case work follow the tRAT, ungraded, but be ready to think out loud.
  • No make-ups. No dropped TBLs.

Lecture exams, 30%

  • 5 exams, one at the close of each module, covering the concept material (the online lectures and in-class recap).
  • Mostly DOK 2–3 (apply and reason), not pure recall.

Lab practical exams, 30%

  • 5 practicals, one per module. Identify anatomy from slides, models, charts, and prosected material.
  • Timed station rotation. Spelling counts. Pen only (no pencil).
  • Doors lock at start, late means you can't enter. Cover your answers; keep eyes on your own station.
  • No make-ups, none dropped.

Lab quizzes, 5%

  • At the end of most labs, one randomly chosen person from each team answers the lab challenge question on that day's material. The whole team earns the point, or doesn't.
  • This rewards everyone contributing, working together, and actually committing the material to memory. If a teammate isn't participating, it is on the team to get them on board.
  • Present earns the team score; absent earns a zero. No make-ups, none dropped.

iChecks & tChecks, 5%

  • The quick check-in questions during class lectures and guided walk-throughs.
  • iCheck = individual, weighted 2×. tCheck = team, weighted 1×.
  • Low-stakes and quick, they reward showing up prepared and paying attention.

Exam cap: exams include built-in bonus questions, but no exam score counts above 102%. This is the only bonus inside exams, there is no other extra credit besides Scholar Points below.

The workload, honestly

Plan about two hours of study for every hour in class.

California community colleges follow the Carnegie standard: for every hour of lecture, expect about two hours of independent study, the classic "three hours of work for one hour of class."

Normally, lab hours carry no outside-study expectation: in a wet lab you run an experiment and leave. Anatomy lab is different. There's no experiment, the lab work is memorization. Slides, models, relationships, cadaver structures, you have to carry all of it in your head into a timed practical. So here, lab and lecture both earn the three-for-one weight. Both are memory-based.

The math for this course: roughly 8 hours a week in class (lecture + lab) → about 15 to 17 hours of focused study outside class → near 24 hours a week total on this one course. Front-load it; anatomy is cumulative and the gap compounds.

Section 8

Scholar Points (up to +3%).

Scholar Points reward steady, proactive learning, not missed work. Applied at the end of the term. Earn the +2% through engagement hours, then add the +1% performance boost on top. Struggling? The Success Sprint (below) opens after Exam 1 as a catch-up path.

Engagement
+2.0%

36 verified hours

Across the term, log 36 hours of tutoring, study-with-me sessions, or faculty review, hosting or attending, in any combination. A maximum of 3 hours per week is counted, so it spreads out instead of piling up.

Reset & catch up
10 hrs

Success Sprint

A free catch-up program that opens after Exam 1 and runs through Exams 2–5, for anyone feeling underwater. You complete a step tied to each of those exams; finish the full sprint to bank 10 of your 36 hours and rebuild your study system. Full steps are posted in Canvas.

Performance
+1.0%

80%+ exam average

Average 80% or higher across all exams. Awarded only if you've also earned the +2% engagement boost.

Built to be reachable. You don't need money or a car: tutoring others counts the same as being tutored, in person counts the same as online, and hosting counts the same as attending. And if you fall behind, the Success Sprint opens after Exam 1 as a 10-hour path back, while the 3-hour weekly cap means students working jobs or caring for family can still get there a little at a time.

No other extra credit. Scholar Points and the in-exam bonus (to 102%) are the only boosts. Please don't ask for more, no exceptions.

Section 10

Attendance and engagement.

Anatomy is cumulative and moves fast. Missing class is costly, and most in-class work cannot be recovered.

  • Week 1 is mandatory. Miss a Week 1 session and your seat may go to the waitlist, you may be dropped.
  • Be present for at least 90% of a session to be counted. Arriving 20 minutes late or leaving 20 minutes early counts as an absence.
  • Missed in-class work cannot be made up, TBLs, exams, lab quizzes, and iChecks/tChecks earn a zero if you're absent.
  • Absences are treated the same whether "excused" or not; you never need to explain one, and the academic result is identical.
  • Doors lock when a lab practical starts. Late means you can't enter.
  • Excessive absence may result in being dropped, at the instructor's discretion and per Solano policy.
  • Check Canvas at least three times a week; turn on announcement notifications.

You'll be on a team for the whole term, come prepared, contribute, and answer team messages within 24 hours.

If you decide to leave, you must formally drop the course yourself. Don't assume it's done for you; not dropping can turn into an avoidable F.

Section 11

Academic integrity and AI.

Cheating and plagiarism are not tolerated. A violation earns at least a zero on the work, may result in an F for the course, and is reported for disciplinary action. Do your own work; cite sources (MLA or APA), ask if you need help.

Using AI

For studying, AI is encouraged, including the "Teach it to an AI" feature in the Mastery OS. For graded work, the rules are strict:

  • Use generative AI only on assignments specifically marked as AI assignments.
  • No AI on exams or on any graded part of a TBL.
  • AI-detection tools may screen submissions. An AI or plagiarism rating above 25% on graded work earns an automatic zero.
  • Unsure whether a tool is allowed on an assignment? Ask before you submit.

See the AI Honor Contract in Canvas for the full requirements.

No late work. No make-ups.

All quizzes, exams, lab work, and assignments are due by their scheduled dates. Late work is not accepted and there are no make-ups. Every in-class assessment is done in person; miss it and you earn a zero. The only built-in cushions are the in-exam bonus (to 102%) and Scholar Points. Section 13

Digital device policy.

Device use in the anatomy lab is strictly regulated. We work with cadaveric human donors under a legal agreement with UCSF's body donation program. The people who donated their bodies to your education cannot consent to being photographed or recorded, and we protect their privacy and dignity.

The rules:

  • No phones, smart watches, AI glasses, tablets, laptops, or recording devices in the lab, ever.
  • Devices silenced and fully out of sight: not in a pocket, lap, or on the table.
  • No digital notes, photos, video, or audio in the lab.
  • Access digital materials outside lab, during open study hours or at home.

Violations may mean immediate removal from the course and further action. Students under 18 must sign the policy, and a parent or guardian must also sign, before working in lab.

Read and sign the agreement in Canvas before your first lab.

Section 14

Your team.

There are three layers of peer learning, use all three:

  • Your TBL team, your home base of 5 to 7 students, balanced by a Week 1 survey and kept the same all term. Graded together on the tRAT; works application and cases together.
  • Study groups, voluntary groups you form to drill structures and run cases outside class.
  • Study-with-me sessions, peer-led review you organize for each other; these count toward Scholar Points engagement hours.

By the end of the first class, exchange contact info with your team and pick one communication platform. The standing expectation is a 24-hour response time. If your team genuinely isn't working, tell me early through Canvas, before it festers.
